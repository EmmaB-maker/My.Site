About Me

My research career began with a fundamental curiosity about how we construct our reality.


This foundation led me to investigate broader questions about what shapes our experience of the world during my BS in Psychology at the University of Washington, where I worked on projects examining how environmental factors and social dynamics influence perception and behavior. Through this work, I developed strong analytical skills in R programming and statistical analysis.


A turning point came when I encountered the concept of active sensing, the idea that perception requires movement. This insight fundamentally shifted my research focus toward embodied cognition. I became fascinated with how motion shapes our experience of space and time, particularly in immersive environments.


Now as a PhD candidate in the VAAST Lab at the University of Utah, I use virtual reality to investigate how movement influences spatial and temporal perception. My current work explores what happens when we manipulate the visual feedback of walking in VR. I study how the brain adapts its perceptual representations when sensory information conflicts with motor output.


My work focuses on how fundamental processes like motion and emotion warp our sense of time passage and distance perceptions. I study how we adapt to changing environments, to understand the human cognition that can explain our behaviors. These are not just lab phenomena, the warping of subjective time and space is a fundamental factor in user engagement and product design. Understanding these perceptual mechanisms has direct applications in creating more intuitive and engaging user experiences.


My approach combines rigorous experimental design with technical implementation, bridging cognitive psychology research with practical application. I'm interested in how fundamental principles of perception can inform better design of VR environments and user interfaces.

The VAAST (Vision, Audition, Action, in Space & Time) Lab at the University of Utah. My research home where we study perception and navigation in virtual and augmented reality environments.
